He has written books and newspaper columns about reasoning and politics.\n\n== Early life and academia ==\nWhyte was born in [[Auckland, New Zealand]]. He received his undergraduate degree from the [[University of Auckland]].<ref>{{cite news | url= http://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/news/article.cfm?c_id=1&objectid=11195695 | title=Jamie Whyte elected Act leader | newspaper=[[The New Zealand Herald]] | date=2 February 2014 | accessdate=18 September 2014 | author=Young, Audrey}}</ref> He then moved to the UK to study for an M.Phil and Ph.D at [[St John's College, Cambridge]].\n\nUpon graduation, Whyte remained at Cambridge University for three years as a research fellow at [[Corpus Christi College, Cambridge|Corpus Christi College]] and temporarily lectured in the Philosophy faculty. He won the ''Analysis'' journal prize for the best article by a philosopher under the age of 30.<ref>{{Cite journal|url = https://philosophynow.org/issues/101/News_March_April_2014|title = Philosopher becomes party leader|last = Roberts|first = Sue|date = March 2014|journal = Philosophy Now|accessdate = 18 September 2014|doi = |pmid = }}</ref>\n\n==Early career==\nWhyte then took up a job with the New York-based management consultancy firm [[Oliver Wyman]].<ref>{{cite web |url= http://www.lse.ac.uk/publicEvents/events/2011/20110726t1830vOT.aspx|title=Keynes v Hayek |date= 8 August 2011| publisher=London School of Economics}}\n</ref> He also worked as a foreign currency trader.\n\n==Writing and media==\nSince 2004, Whyte has written books for general audiences and newspaper columns, aiming to expose shoddy reasoning, especially by politicians. In 2006 he won the [[Reason Foundation]] Bastiat Prize for journalism (jointly with Tim Harford of the ''[[Financial Times]]'') and in 2010 he was named runner up.<ref>Previous Bastiat Prize Winners from [http://reason.org/news/show/1012830.html Reason Foundation], accessed June 2014.</ref> In June 2014, Whyte won the [[Institute of Economic Affairs]] Arthur Seldon Memorial Award for Excellence for ''Quack Policy''.<ref>{{Cite news|url = http://www.iea.org.uk/in-the-media/press-release/political-party-leader-wins-prestigious-seldon-award|title = Political party leader wins prestigious Seldon Award|last = |first = |date = 27 June 2014|work = |accessdate = 18 Sep 2014|publisher = IEA}}</ref>\n\nHe is the author of ''Crimes Against Logic'' (titled ''Bad Thoughts: A Guide to Clear Thinking'' in the UK; 2004), ''A Load of Blair'' (2005), ''Free Thoughts'' (2012) and ''Quack Policy'' (2013) and has also written columns for many notable publications, including ''[[The Times]]'', [[City A.M.|City A.M]]., [[Standpoint (magazine)|Standpoint]], ''Financial Times'' and ''[[The Wall Street Journal]]''.\n\nWhyte has also occasionally broadcast on [[BBC Radio 4]] and television.\n\n== Political career ==\nIn his youth, Whyte was a campaign volunteer for [[Bob Jones (New Zealand)|Bob Jones]]' [[New Zealand Party]] in the [[New Zealand general election, 1984|1984 general election]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/news/article.cfm?c_id=1&objectid=11283858|title=Leaders' first trip to ballot box|author=Cherie Howie|publisher=New Zealand Herald|date=2014-06-29}}</ref>\n\nDespite no prior experience in political office, at a board meeting in February 2014, Whyte was elected to the ACT party's leadership, beating former MP, [[John Boscawen]]. At the same meeting, [[David Seymour (politician)|David Seymour]] was chosen as ACT's candidate for the [[Epsom (New Zealand electorate)|Epsom electorate]]<ref>{{cite web |url= http://www.3news.co.nz/ACT-choices-huge-risk-for-party/tabid/1607/articleID/330702/Default.aspx|title=ACT choices huge risk for party|author=Sabin |date= 2 February 2014| publisher=3 News|first = Brook}}</ref> and Kenneth Wang was later elected as Whyte's deputy leader in April 2014.<ref>{{Cite news|url = http://www.stuff.co.nz/national/politics/9944101/Kenneth-Wang-elected-Act-deputy-leader|title = Kenneth Wang elected Act deputy leader|last = Dastgheib|first = Shabnam|date = 15 April 2014|work = |accessdate = 18 Sep 2014|publisher = Stuff.co.nz}}</ref>\n\nIn the 2014 general election, ACT only won enough votes to be represented by David Seymour in the [[Epsom (New Zealand electorate)|Epsom electorate]].
