He made significant contributions to the emerging genres of reference and travel literature.<ref name=\"google\">{{cite book|title=English Travellers of the Renaissance|author=Howard, C.M.|date=1914|publisher=Burt Franklin Publisher|isbn=9780833717450|url=http://books.google.is/books?id=WHgpr0I7Cm0C|page=26|accessdate=2014-12-07}}</ref> He was the first distinguished representative of a prominent early modern Basel academic family.\n\n==Life and work==\n\nZwinger was the son of the furrier Leonhard Zwinger who had become Basel citizen in 1526. His mother was Christina Herbster, the sister of [[Johannes Oporinus]] (Herbster) the famed humanist printer. After Zwinger’s father’s death, Christina married the noted humanist [[Conrad Lycosthenes]] (Wolffhart).\n\nZwinger studied at universities in [[University of Basel|Basel]], [[University of Lyon|Lyon]], and [[Paris]] before taking a doctorate in medicine at the [[University of Padua]] with [[Bassiano Landi]], the successor of [[Johannes Baptista Montanus]]. In Paris he studied with the iconoclastic philosopher [[Petrus Ramus]]. He joined the faculty of the University of Basel as a member of the ''consilium facultatis medicae'' from 1559. At Basel he held successively chairs in Greek (1565), Ethics (1571), and finally theoretical medicine (1580).<ref>Miescher, Friedrich (1860), ''Die medizinische Facultät in Basel und ihr Aufschwung unter F. Basel: Schweighauser. pp. 18–19.</ref> While originally hostile to [[Paracelsus]], in his later career he took an interest in Paracelsian medical theory for which he experienced some hostility. He associated with Paracelsians such as [[Thomas Moffet]] and [[Petrus Severinus]].<ref>Shackelford, Jole, ''[http://books.google.com/books?id=D8Lf_NBW3TIC&lpg=PA288&ots=FfpPirsURh&dq=petrus%20severinus%20zwinger&pg=PA288#v=onepage&q=petrus%20severinus%20zwinger&f=false A Philosophical Path for Paracelsian Medicine: The Ideas, Intellectual Context, and Influence of Petrus Severinus (1540/2–1602)]'' (Copenhagen: Museum Tusculanum Press, 2004), pp. 287–288.</ref>\n\nZwinger was the editor of the early encyclopedia ''[[Theatrum Humanae Vitae]]'' (editions 1565, 1571, 1586, 1604). The work is considered \"perhaps the most comprehensive collection of knowledge to be compiled by a single individual in the early modern period.\"<ref>Helmut Zedelmaier, \"Navigieren im Text-Universum: Theodor Zwingers Theatrum Vitae Humanae,\" ''Metaphorik'' 14 (2008): 113: \"Theodor Zwingers Theatrum vitae humanae ist die vielleicht umfangreichste Wissenssammlung, die ein einzelner Mensch je in der frühen Neuzeit erstellte.\"</ref> He was able to draw on the knowledge base of his stepfather Conrad Lycosthenes in compiling the ''Theatrum Humanae Vitae''.\n\nZwinger’s son, [[Jakob Zwinger]], briefly served as his successor as editor of the ''Theatrum''.
