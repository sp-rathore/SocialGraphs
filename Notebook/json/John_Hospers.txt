In 1972 he became the first presidential candidate of the [[Libertarian Party (United States)|Libertarian Party]], and was the only [[minor party]] candidate to receive an electoral vote in [[United States presidential election, 1972|that year's U.S. Presidential election]].<ref>{{cite news |author-link=Jesse Walker |last=Walker |first=Jesse |date=June 13, 2011 |title=John Hospers, RIP |work=[[Reason Online]] |access-date=June 14, 2011 |url=http://reason.com/blog/2011/06/13/john-hospers-rip}}</ref>\n\n==Education and career==\nBorn in [[Pella, Iowa]] on June 9, 1918, Hospers graduated from [[Central College (Pella, Iowa)|Central College]]. Hospers earned advanced degrees from the [[University of Iowa]] and [[Columbia University]]. He conducted research, wrote, and taught in areas of [[philosophy]], including [[aesthetics]] and [[ethics]]. He taught philosophy at [[Brooklyn College]] and at the [[University of Southern California]], where for many years he was chairman of the philosophy department and [[professor emeritus]].<ref>[http://johnhospers.com/Hospers.html \"Who Is John Hospers? He appeared on [[Talk radio|radio shows]] with [[Ayn Rand]], and devoted considerable attention to her ideas in his ethics textbook ''Human Conduct''.<ref>{{cite book |title=[[Letters of Ayn Rand]] |publisher=[[E.P. Dutton|Dutton]] |last=Rand |first=Ayn |editor-last=Berliner |editor-first=Michael S. |year=1995 |pages=502–564 |isbn=0-525-93946-6}}</ref>\n\nAccording to Rand's biographer, [[Barbara Branden]], Hospers met Rand when she addressed the student body at Brooklyn College.  They became friends, and had lengthy philosophical conversations.  Rand's discussions with Hospers contributed to her decision to write [[nonfiction]].  Hospers read ''[[Atlas Shrugged]]'' ([[1957 in literature|1957]]), which he considered an aesthetic triumph.<ref>Hospers, John.
