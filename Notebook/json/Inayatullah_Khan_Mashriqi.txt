Shabbir Hussain, Al-Mashriqi: The Disowned Genius, Lahore, [[Jang Group of Newspapers|Jang Publishers]], 1991</ref> \n\n==Education==\nMashriqi had a passion for mathematics from his childhood.<ref name=\"shabbir2\" /> He completed his master's degree in Mathematics from the [[University of the Punjab]] at the age of 19 and broke all previous records. In October 1907 he matriculated at [[Christ's College, Cambridge]], England, to read for the [[Cambridge Mathematical Tripos|mathematics tripos]]. He was awarded a college foundation scholarship in May 1908.<ref>''The Times'', 23 June 1908, page 12.</ref> In June 1909 he was awarded [[British undergraduate degree classification|first class honours]] in Mathematics Part I, being placed joint 27th out of 31 on the list of [[Wrangler (University of Cambridge)|wranglers]].<ref>''The Times'', 16 June 1909, page 9.</ref> For the next two years, he read for the [[Languages of Asia|oriental languages]] tripos in parallel to the natural sciences tripos, gaining first class honours in the former and third class in the latter.<ref>''The Times'',17 June 1911, page 6.</ref><ref>M. Aslam Malik,''Allama Inayatullah Mashraqi'', page 3.</ref>\n\nAfter three years' residence at [[Cambridge]] he had qualified for his Bachelor of Arts degree, which he took in 1910. In 1912 he completed a fourth tripos in mechanical sciences, and was placed in the second class. Following the year, Mashriqi was conferred with DPhil in mathematics receiving a gold medal in his doctoral graduation ceremony.<ref>''The Times'', 13 June 1912, page 7</ref> He left Cambridge and returned to India in December 1912.<ref>M. Aslam Malik,''Allama Inayatullah Mashraqi'', page 4.</ref> During his stay in Cambridge his religious and scientific conviction was inspired by the works and concepts of the professor [[Sir James Jeans]].<ref name=\"shabbir\">S. Shabbir Hussain (ed.), God, Man, and Universe, Akhuwat Publications, Rawalpindi, 1980</ref>{{page needed|date=February 2012}}\n\n==Career==\nOn his return to India, Mashriqi was offered the premiership of [[Alwar]], a [[Princely states|princely state]], by the Raja. He declined owing to his interest in education. At the age of 25 he was appointed Vice Principal of [[Islamia College (Peshawar)|Islamia College]], [[Peshawar]], by Chief Commissioner [[Sir George Roos-Keppel]]. He was made Principal of the same college in 1917. In Oct 1917 he was appointed Under Secretary to the Government of India in the Education Department in succession to Sir George Anderson (1876–1943).<ref>Hira Lal Seth, ''The Khaksar Movement Under Search Light And the Life Story of Its Leader Allama Mashriqi'' (Hero Publications, 1946), p 16</ref> He became headmaster of the High School, Peshawar on 21 October 1919.\n\nIn 1930 he was passed over for a promotion in the government service, following which he went on medical leave. In 1932 he resigned, taking his pension, and settled down in [[Ichhra]], Lahore.<ref>Shan Muhammed, Khaksar Movement in India, Pub. Meenakshi Prakashan, Meerut, 1973</ref>{{page needed|date=February 2012}}\n\n===Nobel nomination===\nIn 1924, at the age of 36, Mashriqi completed the first volume of his book, ''Tazkirah''. It is a commentary on the ''Qur'an'' in the light of science. It was nominated for the Nobel Prize in 1925,<ref>M.Aslam Malik,''Allama Inayatullah Mashraqi''</ref>{{full|date=November 2012}} subject to the condition it was translated into one of the [[European languages]]. Mashriqi, however, declined the suggestion of translation.<ref>[http://www.paktribune.com/news/print.php?id=149500 Allama Mashriqi – a great genius], Pak Tribune, 11 July 2006. (accessed on 30 November 2006)</ref>\n\n===Fellowships===\nMashriqi's fellowships included:<ref name=\"shabbir\"/>{{page needed|date=February 2012}}\n\n*Fellow of the [[Royal Society of Arts]], 1923\n*Fellow of the Geographical Society (F.G.S), Paris\n*Fellow of Society of Arts (F.S.A), Paris\n*Member of the Board at [[Delhi University]]\n*President of the Mathematical Society, [[Islamia College (Peshawar)|Islamia College]], [[Peshawar]]\n*Member of the International Congress of Orientalists ([[Leiden]]), 1930\n*President of the All World's Faiths Conference, 1937\n\n==Mashriqi's philosophy==\n\nHe declared that the science of religions was essentially the science of collective evolution of mankind; all prophets came to unite mankind, not to disrupt it; the basic law of all faiths is the law of unification and consolidation of the entire humanity.<ref name=\"shabbir\"/>{{page needed|date=February 2012}} According to Markus Daeschel, the philosophical ruminations of Mashriqi offer an opportunity to re-evaluate the meaning of colonial modernity and notion of post-colonial [[nation-building]] in modern times.<ref>Markus Daeschel, [http://journals.cambridge.org/action/displayAbstract?fromPage=online&aid=500148 Scientism and its discontents: The Indo-Muslim \"Fascism\" of Inayatullah Khan Al-Mashriqi], ''Modern Intellectual History'', 3: pp. 443–472, [[Cambridge University Press]]. 2006</ref>\n\n==Political life==\nMashriqi is often portrayed as a controversial figure, a religious activist, a revolutionary, and an anarchist; while at the same time he is described as a visionary, a reformer, a leader, and a scientist-philosopher who was born ahead of his time.<ref name=\"shabbir2\" />\n\nAfter Mashriqi resigned from government service, he laid the foundation of the Khaksar Tehrik (also known as Khaksar Movement) in 1930.<ref>Khaksar Tehrik Ki Jiddo Juhad Volume 1. Author Khaksar Sher Zaman</ref>{{full|date=November 2012}}\n[[File:Al-Islah.jpg|thumb|Al-Islah ([[Khaksar Tehrik]] weekly)]]\n\n==Imprisonments and allegations==\n\nOn 20 July 1943, an assassination attempt was made on [[Muhammad Ali Jinnah]] by Rafiq Sabir who was assumed to be a Khaksar worker.<ref>Jinnah of Pakistan, [http://www.humsafar.info/1943.htm Calendar of events, 1943]. Accessed on 2 March 2007</ref> The attack was deplored by Mashriqi, who denied any involvement. Later, Justice Blagden of [[Bombay High Court]], in his ruling on 4 November 1943 dismissed any association of Khaksars.<ref>Akbar A. Peerbhoy, Jinnah Faces An Assassin, Bombay: Thacker & Co., 1943</ref>\n\nIn Pakistan, Mashriqi was imprisoned at least five times: in 1950 prior to election; in 1958 for alleged complicity in the murder of republican leader [[Khan Abdul Jabbar Khan]]; and, in 1962 for suspicion on attempt to overthrow [[Ayub Khan (Field Marshal)|President Ayub's]] government. However, none of the charges were proved, and he was acquitted in each case.<ref name=\"shabbir\"/>{{page needed|date=February 2012}}\n\nIn 1957 Mashriqi allegedly led 300,000 of his followers to the borders of [[Kashmir]], intending, it is said, to launch a fight for its liberation. However, the [[Government of Pakistan|Pakistan government]] persuaded the group to withdraw and the organisation was later disbanded.<ref>Obituary, ''The Times'', 29 August 1963</ref>\n\n==Death==\nMashriqi died on 27 August 1963.\n\n==Mashriqi's works==\nMashriqi's prominent works include:\n\n*''Armughan-i-Hakeem'', a poetical work\n*''Dahulbab'', a poetical work\n*''Isha’arat'', the \"Bible\" of the Khaksar movement\n*''Khitab-e-Misr'' (The Egypt Address), based on his 1925 speech in [[Cairo]] as a delegate to the ''Motmar-e-Khilafat''\n*''Maulvi Ka Ghalat Mazhab''\n*''Tazkirah'' Volume I, 1924, discussions on conflicts between religions, between religion and science, and the need to resolve these conflicts\n*''Tazkirah'' Volume II.
