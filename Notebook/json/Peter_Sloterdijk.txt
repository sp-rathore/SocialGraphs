He is a professor of philosophy and media theory at the [[University of Art and Design Karlsruhe]]. He co-hosted the German television show ''Im Glashaus: Das Philosophische Quartett'' from 2002 until 2012.\n\n== Biography ==\n\nSloterdijk's father is Dutch. He studied philosophy, [[German studies]] and history at the [[University of Munich]] and the [[University of Hamburg]] from 1968 to 1974. In 1975 he received his PhD from the [[University of Hamburg]]. In the 1980s he worked as a freelance writer, and published his ''[[Critique of Cynical Reason|Kritik der zynischen Vernunft]]'' in 1983. He has since published a number of philosophical works acclaimed in Germany. In 2001 he was named chancellor of the University of Art and Design Karlsruhe, part of the [[Center for Art and Media Karlsruhe]]. In 2002 he began to co-host ''Im Glashaus: Das Philosophische Quartett'' (\"In the Glass House:<ref>[http://dict.tu-chemnitz.de/english-german/sits.html from the old German expression ''\"Wer selbst im Glashaus sitzt, sollte nicht mit Steinen werfen\"'' = He who sits in a glass house should not throw stones]</ref> The Philosophical Quartet\"), a show on the German [[ZDF]] television channel devoted to discussing key contemporary issues in-depth.<ref>[http://www.3sat.de/3sat.php?http://www.3sat.de/specials/50919/index.html Im Glashaus – Das philosophische Quartett, ZDF website]</ref>\n\n== Philosophical stance ==\n\nSloterdijk rejects the existence of [[dualism]]s—body and soul, subject and object, culture and nature, etc.—since their interactions, \"spaces of coexistence\", and common technological advancement create hybrid realities. Sloterdijk's ideas are sometimes referred to as [[posthumanism]],<ref>Michael Kempe, \"Neulich im Menschenpark: Die phantastische Anthropologie des Peter Sloterdijk\", in Bernhard Kleeberg ''et alii'' (eds.), ''Die List der Gene: Stratageme eines neuen Menschen'' (Tübingen: Gunter Narr, 2001), pp. 151—170, specifically pp. 162—164.</ref> and seek to integrate different components that have been, in his opinion, erroneously considered detached from each other. Consequently, he proposes the creation of an \"ontological constitution\" that would incorporate all beings—humans, animals, plants, and machines.\n\n== Philosophical style ==\n\nIn the style of [[Nietzsche]], Sloterdijk remains convinced that contemporary philosophers have to think dangerously and let themselves be \"kidnapped\" by contemporary \"hyper-complexities\": they must forsake our present humanist and nationalist world for a wider horizon at once ecological and global.<ref>Book Description for \"Neither Sun Nor Death\", MIT Press 2011</ref>\nSloterdijk's philosophical style strikes a balance between the firm academicism of a scholarly professor and a certain sense of anti-academicism (witness his ongoing interest in the ideas of [[Osho (Bhagwan Shree Rajneesh)|Osho]], of whom he became a disciple in the late seventies).<ref>''[[Die Tageszeitung]]'' [http://www.taz.de/pt/2006/06/13/a0226.1/text interview] dd. 13 June 2006, [http://www.lettre.de/content/hans-j%C3%BCrgen-heinrichs_kantilenen-der-zeit interview] in ''Lettre International'' {{de icon}}</ref> Taking a sociological stance, [[Andreas Dorschel]] sees Sloterdijk's timely innovation at the beginning of the 21st century in having introduced the principles of [[celebrity]] into philosophy.<ref>[http://www.ingentaconnect.com/content/mohr/phr/2013/00000060/00000004/art00002 Andreas Dorschel, \"Denktagebücher: Zur Poetik des philosophischen Journals\", ''Philosophische Rundschau'' LX (2013), no. 4, pp. 264—298, specifically pp. 293—297.]</ref> Sloterdijk himself, viewing exaggeration to be required in order to catch attention, describes the way he presents his ideas as \"hyperbolic\" (''hyperbolisch'').<ref>Holger von Dobeneck, ''Das Sloterdijk-Alphabet: Eine lexikalische Einführung in seinen Ideenkosmos'', 2nd. ed. (Würzburg: Königshausen & Neumann, 2006), p. 10.</ref>\n\n== Individual works ==\n\n=== Critique of Cynical Reason ===\n\nThe ''Kritik der zynischen Vernunft'', published by [[Suhrkamp]] in 1983 (and in English as ''[[Critique of Cynical Reason]]'', 1988), became the best-selling work on philosophy in the German language since the [[Second World War]] and launched Sloterdijk's career as an author.<ref>See Stefan Lorenz Sorgner, [http://www.tabvlarasa.de/20/sorgner.php \"In Search of Lost Cheekiness. An Introduction to Peter Sloterdijk's ''Critique of Cynical Reason''\"], in ''Tabula Rasa'', 2003.</ref>\n\n=== Spheres ===\n\nThe trilogy ''Spheres'' is the philosopher's [[masterpiece|magnum opus]]. The first volume was published in 1998, the second in 1999, and the last in 2004.\n\n''Spheres'' is about \"spaces of coexistence\", spaces commonly overlooked or taken for granted that conceal information crucial to developing an understanding of the human. The exploration of these spheres begins with the basic difference between [[mammal]]s and other animals: the biological and [[utopia]]n comfort of the mother's [[Uterus|womb]], which humans try to recreate through science, ideology, and religion. From these microspheres ([[Ontology|ontological]] relations such as [[fetus]]-[[placenta]]) to macrospheres (''macro-uteri'' such as nations or [[Sovereign state|states]]), Sloterdijk analyzes spheres where humans try but fail to dwell and traces a connection between vital crisis (e.g., emptiness and [[narcissistic]] detachment) and crises created when a sphere shatters.\n\nSloterdijk has said that the first paragraphs of ''Spheres'' are \"the book that [[Martin Heidegger|Heidegger]] should have written\", a companion volume to ''[[Being and Time]]'', namely, \"Being and Space\".{{Citation needed|date=May 2007}} He was referring to his initial exploration of the idea of ''[[Dasein]]'', which is then taken further as Sloterdijk distances himself from Heidegger's positions.<ref>In more recent years, a deepening of this take on [[Heidegger]]'s thought is given expression through a focus on the primordiality of space over time in [[fundamental ontology]] as articulated in numerous studies by the British-Lebanese philosopher and architect [[Nader El-Bizri]] in his investigation of \"the place of being\", of \"dwelling\", and primarily of [[Khôra]].</ref>\n\n=== Globalization ===\n\nSloterdijk also argues that the current concept of [[globalization]] lacks historical perspective. In his view it is merely the third wave in a process of overcoming distances (the first wave being the metaphysical globalization of the [[Greek philosophy|Greek]] [[cosmology]] and the second the nautical globalization of the 15th century). The difference for Sloterdijk is that, while the second wave created [[cosmopolitanism]], the third is creating a global [[Parochialism|provincialism]]. Sloterdijk's sketch of a philosophical history of globalization can be found in ''Im Weltinnenraum des Kapitals'' (2005; translated as ''In the World Interior of Capital''), subtitled \"Die letzte Kugel\" (\"The final sphere\").\n\n=== Rage and Time ===\n\nIn his ''Zorn und Zeit'' (translated as ''Rage and Time''), Sloterdijk characterizes the emotion of rage as a psychopolitical force throughout human history. The political aspects are especially pronounced in the Western tradition, beginning with the opening words of [[Homer]]'s ''Iliad'', \"Of the rage of Achilles, son of Peleus, sing, O Goddess...\". Sloterdijk acknowledges the contributions of [[psychoanalysis]] for our understanding of strong emotional attitudes: \"In conformity with its basic erotodynamic approach, psychoanalysis brought much hatred to light, the other side of life.\" (''Rage and Time'', p.&nbsp;14) Importantly, for Sloterdijk, Judeo-Christian conceptions of God ultimately \"piggyback\" on the feelings of rage and resentment, creating \"metaphysical revenge banks\". For Sloterdijk, \"God thus becomes the location of a transcendent repository of suspended human rage-savings and frozen plans of revenge.\" <ref>[http://berlinbooks.org/brb/2010/12/rage-and-time/ Francesco Klauser in ''The Berlin Review of Books'', December 2010]</ref>\n\n== Genetics dispute ==\n\nShortly after Sloterdijk conducted a symposium on philosophy and Heidegger, he stirred up controversy with his essay \"Regeln für den Menschenpark\" (\"Rules for the Human Park\").<ref>See Frank Mewes, [http://alcor.concordia.ca/~gnosis/vol_vi/sloterdijk.html \"Regulations for the Human Park: On Peter Sloterdijk's Regeln für den Menschenpark\"], in ''Gnosis'', Volume VI, No. 1, 2002.</ref> In this text, Sloterdijk regards cultures and civilizations as \"anthropogenic hothouses,\" installations for the cultivation of human beings; just as we have established wildlife preserves to protect certain animal species, so too ought we to adopt more deliberate policies to ensure the survival of [[Aristotle]]'s ''zoon politikon''.\n\n\"The taming of man has failed\", Sloterdijk laments. \"Civilisation's potential for barbarism is growing; the everyday bestialisation of man is on the increase.\"\n\nBecause of the [[Eugenics|eugenic]] policies of the [[Nazism|Nazis]] in Germany's recent history, such discussions are seen in Germany as carrying a sinister load. Breaking a German taboo on the discussion of genetic manipulation, Sloterdijk's essay suggests that the advent of new genetic technologies requires more forthright discussion and regulation of \"bio-cultural\" reproduction. In the eyes of [[Jürgen Habermas|Habermas]], this made Sloterdijk a \"fascist\". Sloterdijk replied that this was, itself, resorting to \"fascist\" tactics to discredit him.{{Citation needed|date=June 2011}}\n\nThe core of the controversy was not only Sloterdijk's ideas but also his use of the German words ''Züchtung'' (\"breeding\", \"cultivation\") and ''Selektion'' (\"selection\"). Sloterdijk rejected the accusation of Nazism, which he considered alien to his historical context. Still, the paper started a controversy in which Sloterdijk was strongly criticized, both for his alleged usage of a fascist rhetoric to promote [[Plato]]'s vision of a government with absolute control over the population, and for committing a non-normative, simplistic reduction of the bioethical issue itself. This second criticism was based on the vagueness of Sloterdijk's position on how exactly society would be affected by developments in genetic science. After the controversy multiplied positions both for and against him, ''[[Die Zeit]]'' published an open letter from Sloterdijk to Habermas in which he vehemently accused Habermas of \"criticizing behind his back\" and espousing a view of humanism that Sloterdijk had declared dead.<ref>[http://www.independent.co.uk/news/world/anger-as-philosopher-revives-vocabulary-of-third-reich-1123225.html \"Anger as philosopher revives vocabulary of Third Reich\"]</ref>\n\n== Welfare state dispute ==\n\nAnother dispute emerged after Sloterdijk's article \"Die Revolution der gebenden Hand\" (13 June 2009; transl. \"The revolution of the giving hand\")<ref>[http://www.faz.net/s/Rub9A19C8AB8EC84EEF8640E9F05A69B915/Doc~E3E570BE344824089B6549A8283A0933B~ATpl~Ecommon~Scontent.html Peter Sloterdijk \"Die Revolution der gebenden Hand\"]</ref><ref>[http://leninunserallerlehrer.wordpress.com/2009/07/31/die-revolution-der-gebenden-hand-peter-sloterdijk-belehrt-marx-eines-richtigeren/ \"Die Revolution der gebenden Hand – Peter Sloterdijk „belehrt“ Marx eines Richtigeren\"]</ref> in the ''[[Frankfurter Allgemeine]]'', one of Germany's most widely read newspapers. There Sloterdijk claimed that the national [[welfare state]] is a \"fiscal kleptocracy\" that had transformed the country into a \"swamp of resentment\" and degraded its citizens into \"mystified subjects of tax law\".\n\nSloterdijk opened the text with the famous quote of [[leftism|leftist]] critics of capitalism (made famous in the 19th century by [[Pierre-Joseph Proudhon|Proudhon]] in his \"[[What Is Property?]]\") \"[[Property is theft!|Property is theft]]\", stating, however, that it is nowadays the modern [[State (polity)|state]] that is the biggest taker. \"We are living in a fiscal grabbing semi-socialism – and nobody calls for a fiscal civil war.\"<ref>[http://www.berlinonline.de/berliner-zeitung/archiv/.bin/dump.fcgi/2009/1026/feuilleton/0007/index.html \"Wo bleibt der Bürgerkrieg?\"]</ref><ref>[http://www.taz.de/1/debatte/kommentar/artikel/1/blasen-zu-phrasen/ \"Peter Sloterdijk rebelliert gegen den \"Zwangssteuerstaat\" – Blasen zu Phrasen\"]</ref>\n\nHe repeated his statements and stirred up the debate in his articles titled \"Kleptokratie des Staates\" (transl. \"[[Kleptocracy]] of the state\") and \"Aufbruch der Leistungsträger\" (transl. \"Uprising of the performers\") in the German monthly ''Cicero – Magazin für politische Kultur''.<ref>[[:de:Cicero (Zeitschrift)]]</ref><ref>[http://www.cicero.de/97.php?ress_id=1&item=3888 ''Kleptokratie des Staates'', in ''Cicero'', July 2009, p.42]</ref><ref>[http://www.cicero.de/97.php?ress_id=6&item=4370 \"Aufbruch der Leistungsträger'', in ''Cicero'', November 2009, p.94]</ref>\n\nAccording to Sloterdijk, the institutions of the welfare state lend themselves to a system that privileges the marginalized, but relies, unsustainably, on the class of citizens who are materially successful. Sloterdijk's provocative recommendation was that income taxes should be deeply reduced, the difference being made up by donations from the rich in a system that would reward higher givers with social status. Achievers would be praised for their generosity, rather than being made to feel guilty for their success, or resentful of society's dependence on them.<ref>[http://www.globalpost.com/dispatch/germany/100108/welfare-state-peter-sloterdijk \"Germany's welfare state under fire – One of Germany's foremost public intellectuals, Peter Sloterdijk, began the offensive on the welfare state\"]</ref>\n\nIn January 2010, an English translation was published, titled \"A Grasping Hand – The modern democratic state pillages its productive citizens\", in ''[[Forbes]]''<ref>[http://www.forbes.com/2010/01/27/free-market-democracy-modern-opinions-contributors-peter-sloterdijk.html \"A Grasping Hand – The modern democratic state pillages its productive citizens\"]</ref> and in the Winter 2010 issue of ''[[City Journal (New York)|City Journal]]''.<ref>[http://www.city-journal.org/2010/20_1_snd-democratic-state.html \"The Grasping Hand-The modern democratic state pillages its productive citizens\"]</ref>\n\nSloterdijk's 2010 book, ''Die nehmende Hand und die gebende Seite'', contains the texts that triggered the 2009–2010 welfare state dispute.\n\n== Sex and feminism ==\n\nIn September 2016, Sloterdijk published the e-mail novel ''The Schelling Project''.<ref>Peter Sloterdijk, ''Das Schelling-Projekt'' (Berlin: Suhrkamp, 2016)</ref> The semi-autobiographical text contains a self-portrait of the author, appearing as \"Peer Sloterdijk\"; several of Sloterdijk's friends such as Thomas Macho, Siegfried Mauser and Michaela Boenke figure in the novel in slight disguise. Together, so the plot goes, they draft a research proposal to the German Research Funding Agency (Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft) on the evolution of the female [[orgasm]]. To make it look more profound and thus to impress the reviewers, the team fakes a connection of the issue to the metaphysics of the German idealist philosopher [[Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling]]. The reviewers, though, see through the mystification and turn down the application. Following the rejection, the team splits and each of its members takes his or her own course. While Sloterdijk's e-mail novel about an academic hoax was rated mediocre in terms of literary quality,<ref>Jens Jessen in ''DIE ZEIT'', 1 September 2016</ref> it came to be seen more as a political statement, specifically as an attack on gender mainstreaming in 21st century Germany. Critic [[Elke Schmitter]], in a review article for ''Der Spiegel'' under the heading 'Woman as an Old Boys' Joke',<ref>Elke Schmitter, 'Die Frau als Herrenwitz', ''Der Spiegel'', 1 September 2016</ref> described Sloterdijk's text as an anti-feminist pamphlet thinly veiled as a novel.<ref>A similar point is made by Stefanie Lohaus in ''DIE ZEIT'', 23 September 2016</ref> In an interview with [[Süddeutsche Zeitung]] Sloterdijk defended himself against the charges and claimed his attitude towards women to be adoration rather than contempt.<ref>Vera Schroeder/David Pfeifer/Sven Michaelsen, 'Peter Sloterdijk im SZ-Gespräch', ''Süddeutsche Zeitung'', 17 September 2016</ref>\n\n== Honours and awards ==\n\n* 1993: [[Ernst Robert Curtius Prize]] for Essay Writing\n* 2000: [[Friedrich Märker Prize]] for Essay Writing\n* 2001: [[Christian Kellerer Prize]] for the future of philosophical thought\n* 2005: Business Book Award for the [[Financial Times Deutschland]]\n* 2005: [[Sigmund Freud Prize]] for Scientific Prose\n* 2005: [[Austrian Decoration for Science and Art]]<ref>{{cite web | url = http://www.parlament.gv.at/PAKT/VHG/XXIV/AB/AB_10542/imfname_251156.pdf | title = Reply to a parliamentary question | language = German | page=521 | format = pdf | accessdate = 11 December 2012 }}</ref>\n* 2006: Commander of the [[Ordre des Arts et des Lettres]]\n* 2008: Lessing Prize for Criticism\n* 2008: Cicero Prize\n* 2008: International Mendelssohn Prize in Leipzig (category Social Responsibility)\n* 2009: BDA award for architectural criticism\n* 2013: Ludwig-Börne-Prize\n\n== Film appearances ==\n\n* ''[[Marx Reloaded]]'', [[Arte]], April 2011\n\n== List of works ==\n\n=== Works in English translation ===\n\n* ''[[Critique of Cynical Reason]]'', translation by Michael Eldred ; foreword by Andreas Huyssen, Minneapolis, [[University of Minnesota Press]], 1988.
