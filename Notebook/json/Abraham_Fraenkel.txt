He was an early [[Zionist]] and the first Dean of Mathematics at the [[Hebrew University of Jerusalem]]. He is known for his contributions to [[axiomatic set theory]], especially his addition to [[Ernst Zermelo]]'s axioms which resulted in [[Zermelo–Fraenkel axioms]].\n\n==Life==\nFraenkel studied mathematics at the [[University of Munich]], [[University of Berlin]], [[University of Marburg]] and [[University of Breslau]]; after graduating, he lectured at the University of Marburg from 1916, and was promoted to [[professor]] in 1922.\n\nAfter leaving Marburg in 1928, Fraenkel taught at the [[University of Kiel]] for a year. He then made the fateful choice of accepting a position at the [[Hebrew University of Jerusalem]], which had been founded four years earlier, where he spent the rest of his career. He became the first Dean of the Faculty of Mathematics, and for a while served as Rector of the University.\n\nFraenkel was a fervent [[Zionist]] and as such was a member of [[Jewish National Council]] and the Jewish [[Assembly of Representatives (Mandate Palestine)|Assembly of Representatives]] under the [[Mandatory Palestine|British mandate]]. He also belonged to the [[Mizrachi (Religious Zionism)|Mizrachi]] religious wing of Zionism, which promoted Jewish religious education and schools, and which advocated giving the Chief Rabbinate authority over marriage and divorce.\n\n==Mathematician==\nFraenkel's early work was on [[Kurt Hensel]]'s [[p-adic number]]s and on the [[Ring theory|theory]] of [[ring (mathematics)|rings]]. He is best known for his work on [[axiomatic set theory]], publishing his first major work on the topic (\"''Einleitung in die Mengenlehre''\") in 1919. In 1922 and 1925, he published two papers that sought to improve [[Zermelo]]'s axiomatic system; the result is the [[Zermelo–Fraenkel axioms]]. Fraenkel worked in [[set theory]] and [[foundations of mathematics|foundational mathematics]].\n\nFraenkel also was interested in the history of [[mathematics]], writing in 1920 and 1930 about [[Carl Friedrich Gauss|Gauss]]'s works in [[algebra]], and he published a biography of [[Georg Cantor]].
