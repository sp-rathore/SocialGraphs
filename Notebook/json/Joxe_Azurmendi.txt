He has published numerous articles and books on [[ethics]], [[politics]], the philosophy of [[language]], [[Technology|technique]], [[Basque literature]] and [[philosophy]] in general.<ref>[http://www1.euskadi.net/harluxet/hiztegia.asp?sarrera=Azurmendi \"Azurmendi, Joxe\"]. Harluxet Hiztegi Entziklopedikoa (Encyclopedia Online in Basque)(Retrieved 29 October 2013)</ref>\n\nHe is member of [[Jakin (magazine)|Jakin]] and the director of ''Jakin irakurgaiak'', a publishing house which has published over 40 books under his management. He also collaborated with the ''Klasikoak''<ref>[http://www.ei.ehu.es/p289-content/en/contenidos/informacion/euskara_inst_erdaretan/en_erdaret/klasikoak.html ''Klasikoak''], Collection of translations into the Basque language of the classics of universal thought (website of EHU)</ref> publishing firm in the Basque translations of various philosophical works and was one of the founders of ''Udako Euskal Unibertsitatea'' (The Basque Summer University).<ref>Andoni Olariaga: \"Joxe Azurmendi. Askatasunaren pentsalaria\" in Alaitz Aizpuru, ''Eukal Herriko pentsamenduaren gida'', Bilbo: UEU, 2012. p. 147</ref> He is currently a Professor of Modern Philosophy and a lecturer at [[Euskal Herriko Unibertsitatea]] (The University of the Basque Country).<ref>[http://www.ehu.es/ehg/literatura/idazleak/?p=195 Joxe Azurmendi] at The University of the Basque Country (EHU)</ref> In 2010 he was awarded the title \"honorary academic\" by [[Euskaltzaindia]] (The Basque Language Academy).<ref>[http://www.euskaltzaindia.net/index.php?option=com_content&view=article&id=56&Itemid=404&lang=eu Joxe Azurmendi] at The Basque Language Academy</ref>\n\nAzurmendi is an intellectual who studies the problem more than the solution. Azurmendi's essays cover modern European topics in great depth and knowledge. He has incorporated the philosophy and thinking of European thinkers, especially German ones. He often adopts a polemic tone.<ref>Altzibar, Xabier. \"The Essay in Basque\" in Mari Jose Olaziregi (ed.), ''Basque Literary History'', Reno, Center for Basque Studies/University of Nevada, pp.&nbsp;283–84</ref>\n\nIn short, in the opinion of many, Joxe Azurmendi is one of the most prolific and erudite thinkers in the [[Euskal Herria|Basque Country]].<ref>Altzibar, Xabier. \"The Essay in Basque\" in Mari Jose Olaziregi (ed.), ''Basque Literary History'', Reno: Center for Basque Studies/University of Nevada, 2012</ref><ref>Iturriotz, Ander (2010). \"Zergatik Azurmendi?\", ''Hegats'', 45: 9-15.</ref>\n\n==Life==\nJoxe Azurmendi studied [[philosophy]] and [[theology]] at [[Euskal Herriko Unibertsitatea|The University of the Basque Country]], [[Rome]] and [[Münster]].<ref>Preface of Gandiaga in Joxe Azurmendi: ''Hitz berdeak'', Oñati: EFA, 1971</ref>\n\nAt the beginning of the 1960s he joined the cultural movement which grew up around the magazine ''[[Jakin (magazine)|Jakin]]'', and was in fact the director of the publication when it was prohibited for the first time by [[Francisco Franco|Franco's regime]]. He has collaborated closely and uninterruptedly with the magazine since its restoration. In that publication he has raised the problems of Basque society in the context of European thinkers.<ref>Sudupe, Pako: ''50eko hamarkadako euskal literatura II'', Donostia: Utriusque Vasconiae, 2011. p. 283.</ref> During the early 1970s he focused his attention on disseminating basic literature in the [[Euskara|Basque language]] on subjects which were being hotly debated at the time in the Basque Country: [[nationhood]], [[socialism]], [[Internationalism (politics)|internationalism]],<ref>Joxe Azurmendi: ''Arana Goiri-ren pentsamendu politikoa'', Donostia: Hordago Lur, 1979. p. 1.</ref> etc. In the 1980s he began teaching at [[Euskal Herriko Unibertsitatea|The University of the Basque Country]], and in 1984 he submitted his thesis on [[Jose Maria Arizmendiarrieta]], the founder of the [[Mondragon Corporation|Mondragon cooperative movement]], in which he argued that Arizmendiarrieta's project aimed to unite [[Person|individuals]] and [[society]] under an organisation which combined both [[socialism]] and French [[personalism]].<ref>Andoni Olariaga: \"Joxe Azurmendi. Askatasunaren pentsalaria\" in Alaitz Aizpuru, ''Euskal Herriko pentsamenduaren gida'', Bilbo: UEU, 2012. p. 149</ref>\n\nIn 1992 he published what was to become his best-known work: ''Espainolak eta euskaldunak'' (The Spanish and the Basques). The work, published by Elkar, was written in response to a text by [[Claudio Sánchez-Albornoz|Sánchez-Albornoz]] which claimed that \"The Basques are the last people to be civilised in Spain; they have a thousand years less civilisation than any other people&nbsp;... They are rough, simple people who nevertheless consider themselves to be the children of God and the heirs to his glory. But they are really nothing more than un-Romanised Spaniards.\"<ref>Joxe Azurmendi: ''Espainolak eta euskaldunak'', Donostia: Elkar, 1992. p. 17.</ref> Azurmendi's essay refuted and dismantled the stereotypes maintained about the Basques by certain Spanish intellectuals.\n\nIt was on the threshold of the new millennium, however, that Azurmendi's work reached its height. During the early years of the 21st century he published the trilogy formed by ''Espainiaren arimaz'' (About the soul of Spain) (2006, Elkar), ''Humboldt. Hizkuntza eta pentsamendua'' (Humboldt. Language and Thought) (2007, UEU) and ''Volksgeist. Herri gogoa'' (Volksgeist. National Character) (2008, Elkar). In this trilogy, Joxe Azurmendi reveals some of his most significant thinking.\n\n==Thinking==\nHis work emerged and developed during a period marked by a crisis of culture, politics and values. But it was a crisis that he understood not as something negative, but rather something that opened up a whole new range of possibilities. Consequently, all his thinking is centred around the defence of [[Liberty|freedom]] in every field, but especially in relation to [[conscience]] and [[thinking]].\n\nFar from fleeing the crisis, then, his work tries to outline how we can live in this situation. To this end, he adopts a [[relativism|relativist]] perspective, and given that [[modernity]] has left us with no solid base, he fights against the last vestiges of the [[dogmatism]] towards which our society tends to lean when in crisis:\n{{quote|\" ''The proclamation of relativism is provocative.&nbsp;... I am not particularly interested in being an apostle of relativism. But as I come from a dogmatic culture [Franco's regime], I'm allergic to some things. Truth, Reason and absolute correction were Catholic in that culture. Now I hear that postmodern relativism is the cause of the moral misery and the loss of values. It is seen that there is a nostalgia of dogmatic culture, disguised with some democratic and enlightened discourse. That dogmatic culture has relativism as its enemy, for that reason I claim this convicted relativism. But it is not an absolute relativism.''\"<ref>Joxe Azurmendi: \"The Basques are Argonauts with very fragile ships\", Interview in ''Hegats'' (45), p. 209.</ref>}}\nIn this sense, for example, he is critical of the modern [[State (polity)|state]], which he accuses of being the new [[Christian Church|church]] seeking to control our consciences.<ref>Joxe Azurmendi: ''Barkamena, kondena, tortura'', Donostia: Elkar, 2012.</ref> He also criticises the exploitation of [[morality]], or in other words, how politicians, instead of solving the problems facing them in their various areas or fields, flee instead to moral ground in order to hide their responsibilities under the cloak of supposedly absolute moral principles:\n{{quote|\"''In the mean time, what is the point of repeating the old tale as to what the state is becoming? Once the sour critical analysis of sometime ago ([[Herbert Marcuse]]: ''[[One-Dimensional Man]]''), the dark negative utopias ([[Aldous Huxley]], [[George Orwell]]) and the protest cries ([[May 68]]) are forgotten, and with a near lack of the slightest sense of resistance in civil society, the cobweb of power spins peacefully over our heads, all over the place. Even the dressing room.''\"<ref>Joxe Azurmendi: ''Demokratak eta biolentoak'',Donostia: Elkar, 1997. p. 101.</ref>}}\n\nHe has also made an important contribution in questioning the canonical interpretations which have been constructed regarding different issues. Of particular interest, due to his erudition and training in Germany, is his interpretation of the German [[Age of Enlightenment|Enlightenment]]. In this context he deconstructs the apparent opposition between the French [[Age of Enlightenment|Enlightenment]] and German [[Romanticism]] and proposes a new way of thinking about the different aspects which stem from this opposition.<ref>Joxe Azurmendi: ''Volksgeist. Herri gogoa'', Donostia: Elkar, 2008. p. 129.</ref> In this way, he defies certain Spanish and French intellectuals ([[Alain Finkielkraut]]<ref>Joxe Azurmendi: ''Euskal Herria krisian'', Donostia: Elkar, 1999. p.119</ref>) and argues that [[nationalism]] in fact arose in France ([[Montesquieu]], [[Voltaire]], [[Rousseau]], [[Ernest Renan]]) and was later reinterpreted by the German thinkers and romantics. By doing this, he questions the way in which authors such as [[Johann Wolfgang von Goethe|Goethe]], [[Friedrich Schiller|Schiller]], [[Johann Gottfried Herder|Herder]] or [[Wilhelm von Humboldt|Humboldt]] are viewed as the fathers of [[metaphysics|metaphysical]] nationalism. In this field, the opposition between [[civic nationalism]] and [[ethnic nationalism]] is deconstructed.<ref>Joxe Azurmendi: ''Historia, arraza, nazioa'', Donostia: Elkar, 2014. p. 27.</ref> Thus, Azurmendi criticizes the [[Essentialism|essentialist]] basis of Spanish nationalism<ref>Joxe Azurmendi: ''Espainiaren arimaz'', Donostia: Elkar, 2006</ref> and French nationalism<ref>Joxe Azurmendi: ''Historia, arraza, nazioa'', Donostia: Elkar, 2014</ref> that operates under these [[nation state]]s.\n\nSome of the topics Azurmendi deeply develops in his essays first appeared in his poetry of youth. Azurmendi is within the Basque poetry of the 60s which shows the fight against the tradition, the old faith and the dogmatic certainties:<ref>Estankona, I. 2010: \"Artean gazte eta berde: Azurmendiren ''Hitz berdeak'' eta ''Manifestu atzeratua''\", ''Hegats'', 45: 71-83</ref>\n\n<poem style=\"margin-left: 2em;\">\nBut we wish to be free\nis that my fault?\nThey tried to give us a tree from [[Gernikako Arbola|Gernika]],\na false blank check,\nas if the desire to be free were a sin,\nbut despite that, we, quite simply, wish to be free.\nThat is what we want, that is all.\nThis is the latest deception:\nthey have led us to believe\nbefore from outside and now from within\nthat it is our responsibility to justify our wish to be free.\n\n''Manifestu atzeratua'' (Belated Manifesto) (1968)\n</poem>\n\n\nHe also dedicates a large part of his work to recovering and reinterpreting Basque thinkers, breaking through and dismantling numerous stereotypes. Of particular interest is his research into [[Jon Mirande]], [[Nikolas Ormaetxea|Orixe]], [[Unamuno]]<ref>Joxe Azurmendi: \"Unamunoren atarian\" in ''Euskal Herriko pentsamenduaren gida'', Bilbo: UEU, 2012. p. 29.</ref><ref>Joxe Azurmendi: ''Bakea Gudan. Unamuno, historia eta karlismoa'', Tafalla: Txalaparta, 2012. Azurmendi delves into the thinking of Unamuno, under the pretext of the novel ''Paz en la guerra'' by Unamuno, which deals with the last Carlist War and especially with the site of Bilbo. It therefore turned out in an interpretive essay on the conflict [[Carlism]] vs. [[liberalism]], or in other words, in an interpretive essay on Basque Country and Modernity. According to Azurmendi Unamuno analyzed and rejected the Basque problem from a 19th-century point of view</ref><ref>Interview on Unamuno: [http://paperekoa.berria.info/plaza/2012-05-31/032/001/unamuno_ez_da_inoiz_liberala_izan_antiliberala_zen_azken_fasean_ere.htm «Unamuno ez da inoiz liberala izan; antiliberala zen azken fasean ere»], ''Berria'', 31 May 2012 (Retrieved 29 October 2013)</ref> and others. He is an author who has worked from within and for Basque culture. He claims to have been influenced by Basque authors<ref>Joxe Azurmendi: \"Kierkegaard-en 'egunkari ezkutua'\", in J.L. Ormaetxea, ''Txillardegi lagun giroan'', Bilbo: UEU, 2000</ref> from the post-war period, for example, in questions of language. In this field, he has researched other authors also, including [[Heidegger]], [[Wittgenstein]], [[George Steiner]] and [[Wilhelm von Humboldt|Humboldt]]. The fact that his vast oeuvre is all written in the [[Basque language]] is clearly consistent with his thinking.\n\n==Writing style==\nIn his language Joxe Azurmendi combines an educated register with colloquial expressions, and his prose is fast, incisive, and ironic. Azurmendi's [[Basque language|Basque]] is modern and standard and he demonstrates great knowledge of the language, and richness and variety of expression.<ref>Altzibar, Xabier 2012. \"The Essay in Basque\", in Mari Jose Olaziregi, ''Basque Literary History'', Reno: Center for Basque Studies/University of Nevada. p. 284</ref>\n\n==Works==\nThe ''Inguma''<ref>[http://www.inguma.org/egiledatuakgakoa.php?egile_gakoa=1102&hizkuntza=eu Joxe Azurmendi]</ref> database of the Basque scientific community contains over 180 texts written by Azurmendi.\n\n===Essays===\n* ''Hizkuntza, etnia eta marxismoa'' (Language, Ethnics and Marxism) (1971, Euskal Elkargoa)\n* ''Kolakowski'' ([[Leszek Kołakowski|Kołakowski]]) (1972, EFA): co-author: Joseba Arregui\n* ''Kultura proletarioaz'' (About Proletarian Culture) (1973, Jakin EFA)\n* ''Iraultza sobietarra eta literatura'' (The Soviet Revolution and Literature) (1975, Gero Mensajero)\n* ''Gizona Abere hutsa da'' (Man is Pure Animal) (1975, EFA)\n* ''Zer dugu Orixeren kontra?''
