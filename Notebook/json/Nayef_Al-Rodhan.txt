F. Al-Rodhan''' ({{lang-ar|نايف الروضان}}; born 1959) is a Saudi [[Philosophy|philosopher]], [[Neuroscience|neuroscientist]], [[Geostrategy|geostrategist]], and author. He is an Honorary Fellow of [[St. Antony’s College, Oxford|St. Antony’s College]] at Oxford University, Oxford, United Kingdom,<ref>[http://www.sant.ox.ac.uk/mec/fellows.html St. Antony’s College, University of Oxford]</ref> Senior Fellow and Centre Director of the Geopolitics and Global Futures Programme at the [[Geneva Centre for Security Policy]], Geneva, Switzerland.\n\n==Biography==\nThe [[Saudi Arabia]]n Professor Nayef Al-Rodhan began his career as a [[Neurosurgery|neurosurgeon]] and [[Neuroscience|neuroscientist]]. As a medical student, he was mentored and influenced by the renowned [[neurologist]], [[John Walton, Baron Walton of Detchant|Lord John Walton of Detchant]]. He trained in [[neurosurgery]] and conducted [[neuroscience]] research at the [[Mayo Clinic]], [[Rochester, Minnesota]] in the United States. He became Chief Resident in [[neurosurgery]] and was influenced by Thoralf M. Sundt, David Piepgrass, and Patrick J Kelly at the [[Mayo Clinic]]. He obtained a Ph.D. in 1988 for his work on the ''Characterization of Opioid and Neurotensin Receptor Subtypes in the Brain with Respect to Antinociception''.<ref>[http://www.worldcat.org/title/characterization-of-opioid-and-neurotensin-receptor-subtypes-in-the-brain-with-respect-to-antinociception/oclc/170883051&referer=brief_results Characterization of opioid and neurotensin receptor subtypes in the brain with respect to antinociception, Thesis (Ph.D.) - Pharmacology - Mayo Graduate School of Medicine, 1988]</ref>\n\nIn 1993, on a fellowship from the [[Congress of Neurological Surgeons]], he joined the department of [[neurosurgery]] at the [[Yale University School of Medicine]] as a fellow in [[epilepsy]] surgery and molecular neuroscience under the direction of Dennis Spencer.<ref>[http://epilepsy.yale.edu/education/fellowships/neurosurgery/alumni.aspx Yale Epilepsy Program]</ref>\n\nIn 1994, Nayef Al-Rodhan became a fellow at the department of [[neurosurgery]] at the [[Massachusetts General Hospital]] at [[Harvard Medical School]], where he worked on the study of [[neuropeptide]]s, [[molecular genetics]], and [[neuronal]] [[Regeneration (biology)|regeneration]]. In 1995, he was appointed to the faculty of the [[Harvard Medical School]] and while at Harvard and [[Massachusetts General Hospital]], he founded the [[neurotechnology]] program with [[Nobel Prize]] winner [[James E. Muller]]. Working with Robert Martuza, Al-Rodhan also founded the Laboratories for Cellular Neurosurgery and Neurosurgical Technology at the department of [[neurosurgery]] of Massachusetts General Hospital, Harvard Medical School.<ref>[http://cnsr.mgh.harvard.edu/info.html Harvard Medical School]</ref><ref>[http://www.cimit.org/news/neurotechnology.html Center for Integration of Medicine & Innovative scholarly focus to the interplay between [[neuroscience]] and [[international relations]].<ref>[http://www.thenortherntimes.com.au/news/lifestyle/entertainment/books/neostatecraft-and-metageopolitics-lit-books-switzerland/1991055.aspx The Northern Times]</ref> Through several trends in contemporary [[geopolitics]], [[global security]], [[national security]], [[Transculturation|transcultural]] security, and [[war]] and [[peace]].<ref>[http://www.americanchronicle.com/articles/view/194197 American Chronicle]</ref>\n\nIn 2006, Nayef Al-Rodhan joined the [[Geneva Center for Security Policy]] in Geneva, Switzerland, as and their current and future regional and global geopolitical relevance.<ref>[http://www.sant.ox.ac.uk/mec/fellows.html St. Antony’s College, University of Oxford]</ref>\nIn 2014, he became an
